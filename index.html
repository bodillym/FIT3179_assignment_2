Skin Cancer Prevalence in Australia

The following visualisation explores the prevalence of skin cancer in Australia. The visualisation specifically looks at how skin cancer diagnoses have changed over time, as well as how location, gender, and age influence melanoma of the skin prevalence and survival.

The data used spans a 34 year period from January 1982 to December 2016.

The bar chart on the right shows the leading forms of cancer amongst Australians, with melanoma of the skin being in the top five of all cancer diagnoses for both genders.

Skin Cancer Over Time

The line chart above shows how the yearly number of melanoma of the skin diagnoses has drastically increased from 1982 to 2016.

The diverging bar chart on the left shows how this increase in incidences over time has also led to an increase in deaths. We also see how males experience significantly more deaths than females, where there is almost double the amount of male deaths.

Impact of Location on Skin Cancer

The UV index is used to indicate when UV radiation from the sun is at dangerous levels. The heat map on the right explores the average daily UV index per month in popular Australian cities. Alice Springs is found to have the overall highest UV indices, and Kingston is found to have the lowest. The heat map also demonstrates how the UV index is significantly lower in the winter season.

The scale below can be used as a UV index reference.

Additonally, the choropleth map below explores the percentage of incidences per state in 2016. We see that Queensland has the highest percentage of incidences.

Factors that Influence Survival

The gender of a patient, age of a patient, stage of the cancer and period of time elapsed since diagnosis all impact a patient's chance of survival.

The connected dot plot to the right shows how survival rates decreases over time. It also shows how males typically have a lower survival rate than females.

The bar charts below compare the survival rates one year after diagnosis to three years after diagnosis for differing age groups. We see that stage 1 cancer has a survival rate of almost 100%, regardless of the age group and year from diagnosis. However, we also see that survival decreases for higher stages, and especially for higher age groups.
